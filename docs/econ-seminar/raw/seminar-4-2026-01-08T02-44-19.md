## **SEMINAR PRESENTATION**

### **"Information Asymmetry and Bid-Ask Spreads: Evidence from VPIN and Market Microstructure"**


---


**THESIS & FALSIFIABLE PREDICTION (ex-ante):**

I claim that *information asymmetry, as measured by VPIN (Volume-synchronized Probability of Informed Trading), is positively correlated with effective bid-ask spreads in U.S. equity markets*. The mechanism is specific: market makers widen spreads to protect against adverse selection risk when informed traders are more likely to be active. My falsifiable prediction is: **if VPIN increases, measured bid-ask spreads should widen proportionally; if spreads remain stable despite VPIN increases, the mechanism does not operate**. I am making a narrow claim about correlation in specific microstructure context, NOT claiming this explains all spread variation, affects asset pricing, or determines market efficiency broadly.

**SUPPORTING EVIDENCE:**

VPIN is a quantified measure of informed trading probability, updated in volume-time using tick-by-tick order flow imbalances (Easley, López de Prado, O'Hara 2012). Empirical studies document positive correlations between VPIN and short-term volatility (Andersen et al.) and between information asymmetry measures (PIN/VPIN) and bid-ask spreads across multiple equity markets. The mechanism is straightforward: when VPIN signals higher informed trading probability, market makers face greater adverse selection risk and widen spreads to compensate. Bartram's research comparing options markets with different adverse selection levels shows spreads are 50%+ wider in markets facing acute adverse selection, supporting the theoretical link.

**CRITICAL LIMITATIONS & MEASUREMENT ISSUES:**

I must be explicit about what I cannot claim. First, **VPIN itself is imperfectly measured**—it depends on order flow classification algorithms that may misidentify informed versus uninformed trades, especially during volatile periods. Second, **causation is unclear**—do VPIN increases cause spread widening, or do both respond simultaneously to an underlying latent information shock? Third, **I have not tested whether the correlation is economically significant**—a 5% VPIN increase might correlate with a 0.1 basis point spread widening, which is measurable but economically irrelevant. Fourth, **the relationship varies by market structure**: high-frequency trading, market maker competition, and exchange technology all modulate the VPIN-spread relationship, so findings from one market may not generalize. Finally, **bid-ask spreads themselves are ambiguously measured**—effective spreads (realized transaction costs) differ from quoted spreads and vary with order size, time of day, and stock characteristics.

**SCOPE BOUNDARIES:**

This finding applies to: large-cap U.S. equities with continuous trading and measurable order flow; periods where VPIN calculation is reliable (normal market conditions, not flash crashes); and contemporaneous relationships (not predictive claims about future spread behavior). I cannot defend claims about: small-cap stocks with thin order books, options markets with different structure, or how spread width affects investor welfare. The correlation I've documented says nothing about whether information asymmetry "causes" spreads in a causal sense or whether spread width is "too wide" or "efficiently priced."
