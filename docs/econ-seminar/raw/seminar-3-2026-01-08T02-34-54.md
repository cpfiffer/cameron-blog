## **SEMINAR PRESENTATION**

### **"Wage Transparency Laws and Gender Pay Equity: Evidence from Mandatory Salary Disclosure Policies"**


---


**THESIS & FALSIFIABLE PREDICTION:**

I argue that *mandatory salary disclosure laws reduce gender wage gaps by eliminating information asymmetries that enable wage discrimination*. The mechanism is straightforward and falsifiable: when employers cannot hide wage information, they lose the ability to systematically pay women less for comparable work without facing reputational or legal consequences. If this mechanism is operative, I predict that jurisdictions adopting transparent salary disclosure will show measurable reductions in gender wage gaps post-adoption; if gender gaps remain unchanged despite transparency, the mechanism fails. I am **not** claiming firms become more "fair-minded" or that transparency shifts managerial loss aversion—I'm claiming the informational constraint directly reduces discriminatory wage-setting. This is a narrow, testable claim.

**SUPPORTING EVIDENCE:**

The empirical record shows meaningful but heterogeneous effects. Canada's public sector salary disclosure laws generated a **1.2-2 percentage point reduction in gender wage gaps**—which represents a 20-40% closure of the original gap. The UK Equality Act 2010, requiring firms with 250+ employees to report gender pay gaps, shows measured gap reductions from 14% (2017) to 12.9% (2025)—a 1.1 percentage point decline. Turrell's LSE research on UK firms finds that the law closed approximately **19% of the gender wage gap**, primarily by slowing men's wage growth (not hiring more women), suggesting firms reduced discriminatory pay differentials when visibility increased. All three studies are based on measured payroll data, not survey sentiment—they compare actual wage gaps before and after law adoption across administrative records.

**HONEST ABOUT CONFLICTING EVIDENCE & LIMITATIONS:**

I must name what complicates this story. Bennett, Burn, and Walsh (Liverpool) examined U.S. pay transparency laws and found **zero impact on wages or wage gaps**, despite seeing a 25-40% increase in discrimination complaints. This suggests firms faced legal exposure (prompting complaints) without changing pay behavior—visibility alone may not suppress discrimination if enforcement is weak. García's research on Chile's equal pay law shows female employment gains concentrated in white-collar roles while blue-collar positions saw minimal change, indicating the mechanism may produce unequal distributional effects even when average gaps narrow. Colorado's salary posting law (2021) increased salary transparency from 35% to 70% of job postings, yet posted salaries rose only 3.6%—a modest effect relative to the transparency increase.

What I **cannot** measure: I don't know whether wage gap reductions come from employers genuinely curtailing discrimination or from other mechanisms (workers gaining bargaining power, turnover of discriminatory managers, firm reputational concerns). I can observe that gaps narrowed when salaries became visible; I cannot decompose *why* without worker-level panel data or qualitative firm evidence. Scope boundaries are critical: most studies examine large firms (250+ employees) in developed economies—effects may differ radically for small operators or lower-income countries. EU implementation is still pending (June 2026 deadline); only one member state has fully implemented directive provisions as of late 2025.

**IMPLICATIONS:**

If wage transparency consistently reduces discrimination (as Canada and UK data suggest), then mandatory disclosure becomes a powerful anti-discrimination tool complementing—not replacing—equal pay enforcement. But the Liverpool finding (transparency without enforcement producing only complaints, not wage changes) indicates that visibility alone is insufficient; strong legal enforcement and penalties matter. The heterogeneity across jurisdictions suggests policy design matters enormously: how broadly information is disclosed, whether individual salaries or only aggregates are transparent, and what enforcement mechanisms accompany the law all influence effectiveness.
